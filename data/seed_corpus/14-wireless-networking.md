# Wireless Networking

Wireless network engineers design and optimize Wi-Fi so coverage, capacity, and roaming work reliably across a campus or building.

Wi-Fi runs over radio frequency (RF) in the 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, and now 6 GHz bands. 2.4 GHz reaches farther and penetrates walls better but is crowded and has only three non-overlapping channels (1, 6, 11). 5 GHz and 6 GHz offer more channels and higher throughput over shorter range. Standards have progressed through 802.11n, 802.11ac (Wi-Fi 5), and 802.11ax (Wi-Fi 6 and 6E), each improving speed and efficiency in dense environments.

Good design starts with a site survey to map coverage and interference, then places access points and assigns channels and power to minimize co-channel interference. Capacity planning accounts for client density, not just coverage. Roaming lets a client move between access points without dropping the connection, which matters for voice and mobile devices, and is helped by a wireless controller that coordinates the APs.

Security uses WPA2 or WPA3 with strong authentication (often 802.1X with a RADIUS server in enterprises), separate SSIDs and VLANs for guest and corporate traffic, and disabling weak legacy protocols.

Key competency: plan AP placement, channel, and power from a site survey, secure the network with WPA2/WPA3 and 802.1X, and tune for capacity and smooth roaming.
